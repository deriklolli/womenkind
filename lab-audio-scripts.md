## HORMONE PANEL

### FSH — Follicle-Stimulating Hormone
FSH is a hormone your brain produces to signal your ovaries. Think of it as your brain asking, "Are you ready?" As menopause approaches, your ovaries become less responsive to that signal — and your brain starts asking louder and louder. A higher FSH number is your body's way of confirming that the menopausal transition is underway. It's one of the most reliable markers we have.

### LH — Luteinizing Hormone
LH is a companion hormone to FSH — they work as a pair. Together, they used to coordinate your monthly cycle. As menopause approaches, both tend to rise. When your doctor looks at your results, they often look at FSH and LH together, because the relationship between the two can help confirm what's happening hormonally.

### E2 — Estradiol
Estradiol is the main form of estrogen your ovaries produce, and it's really the one doing most of the work. Hot flashes, sleep trouble, mood changes, dry skin — a lot of what you feel during menopause traces back to estradiol dropping. This number helps your provider understand how far along that change is, and whether hormone therapy might help.

### TESTO — Testosterone
Yes, women have testosterone too — and it matters more than most people realize. It supports your energy, your sex drive, your mood, and your ability to maintain muscle. It declines gradually through perimenopause, and when it gets low, you might notice fatigue, low motivation, or just not feeling like yourself. This number helps your provider see if that's a factor for you.

### PROG — Progesterone
Progesterone is the hormone that rises after ovulation each month. As cycles become irregular in perimenopause, progesterone production gets unpredictable. Low levels can affect sleep and contribute to anxiety. It's also important if you're on estrogen therapy — progesterone is what protects the uterine lining and keeps that balance safe.

### DHEA-S
DHEA is made by your adrenal glands and acts as a building block for both estrogen and testosterone. It declines naturally with age, and more so during menopause. When it's low, people often notice fatigue, lower libido, and just a general dulling of energy and mood. It's a useful piece of the overall hormonal picture.

## THYROID PANEL

### TSH — Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone
Your thyroid controls your metabolism — your energy, your weight, your temperature regulation. TSH is the signal that tells it how hard to work. We check this because an underactive or overactive thyroid can cause fatigue, weight changes, and mood swings that look almost identical to menopausal symptoms. Making sure your thyroid is working well is an important first step.

### FT4 — Free T4
T4 is the main hormone your thyroid produces, and this test measures the portion that's actively available in your bloodstream. It's checked alongside TSH to get a clearer picture — because sometimes TSH looks normal while T4 tells a different story. It helps your provider distinguish between different types of thyroid function issues.

### FT3 — Free T3
T3 is the most active thyroid hormone — it's what your cells actually use. Your body converts T4 into T3, but that conversion can slow down with age or stress. When T3 is low even though everything else looks fine, it can cause fatigue and brain fog that's easy to mistake for menopause. This number helps catch that.

## LIPID PANEL

### TCHOL — Total Cholesterol
Total cholesterol is a summary number — it captures all the different types of cholesterol in your blood at once. On its own it's a starting point, not the whole story. What matters more is the breakdown between the protective kind and the kind that builds up in arteries. Your provider looks at this alongside the other cholesterol numbers to get the full picture.

### LDL — LDL Cholesterol
LDL is often called the "bad" cholesterol — it's the type that can slowly build up in artery walls. Before menopause, estrogen helped keep LDL in check. Once estrogen declines, LDL often rises. Keeping an eye on it matters because elevated LDL is one of the main factors in cardiovascular risk, which increases after menopause.

### HDL — HDL Cholesterol
HDL is the "good" cholesterol — it actually helps carry other cholesterol out of your arteries and back to the liver. Higher is better. Menopause can gradually lower HDL, which is worth watching. When HDL is strong, it's genuinely protective for your heart, so your provider will look at whether lifestyle or treatment changes could help keep it up.

### TRIG — Triglycerides
Triglycerides are a type of fat that circulates in your blood, largely influenced by what you eat and how your metabolism is working. They tend to creep up during and after menopause. High triglycerides on their own raise cardiovascular risk, but the concern is bigger when they're elevated alongside LDL or low HDL — your provider looks at all of them together.

## METABOLIC PANEL

### GLU — Fasting Glucose
Fasting glucose is a snapshot of your blood sugar after not eating overnight. During menopause, hormonal shifts can make it harder for your body to manage blood sugar efficiently. A slightly elevated fasting glucose is often the first sign that insulin resistance is developing — which is common, very manageable, and much easier to address when caught early.

### HBA1C — Hemoglobin A1c
Where fasting glucose is a snapshot, A1c gives you a three-month average. It tells us how your blood sugar has been running over time, not just today. This is especially useful because blood sugar can fluctuate day to day. An elevated A1c can indicate prediabetes, which becomes more common after menopause and is very responsive to early intervention.

### INS — Fasting Insulin
This test measures how much insulin your body is producing to keep your blood sugar in check. Here's the important part: insulin can be elevated even when blood sugar still looks normal. That means fasting insulin can catch early insulin resistance — a metabolic shift that often accelerates during menopause — before it shows up anywhere else.

### BUN — Blood Urea Nitrogen
BUN is a measure of how well your kidneys are clearing waste from your blood. It's part of a standard metabolic baseline. For most people the number will be normal, and that's reassuring. But it's worth checking, especially before starting new medications, because kidney function affects how the body processes and eliminates drugs — including hormones.

### CREAT — Creatinine
Creatinine is a byproduct of normal muscle activity, and your kidneys filter it out. The level in your blood tells us how efficiently your kidneys are working. Like BUN, it's a baseline check — most results are normal and that's the point. It gives your provider confidence that your kidneys are healthy before adjusting or starting any treatments.

## BONE HEALTH

### VITD — Vitamin D
Vitamin D is essential for absorbing calcium and keeping bones strong. It also plays a role in mood and immune function. Deficiency is extremely common — many people don't know they're low until they test. After menopause, when bone loss naturally accelerates, having good vitamin D levels becomes especially important. It's one of the most straightforward things to correct if it's low.

### CA — Calcium
Almost all the calcium in your body lives in your bones and teeth — this test measures the small amount circulating in your blood. Blood calcium is tightly regulated, so if it's off, it usually points to something worth investigating, like a parathyroid issue. On its own it's not a direct measure of bone density, but it's an important piece of the bone health picture.

### CTX — Bone Turnover Marker
CTX measures how fast your bones are currently breaking down. Bone is constantly being rebuilt and resorbed — that's normal. But after menopause, the breakdown can outpace the rebuilding, leading to bone loss. A high CTX tells your provider that process is happening faster than it should, which can help them decide if bone-protective treatment is something to consider sooner rather than later.

## BLOOD & IRON

### CBC — Complete Blood Count
A complete blood count is a broad snapshot of your blood health — it looks at red cells, white cells, and platelets all at once. During perimenopause, heavy or irregular periods can quietly cause anemia without you realizing it. This test helps catch that, along with screening for anything else in the blood that might need attention.

### WBC — White Blood Cells
White blood cells are your immune system's frontline defense. This count tells us whether your immune system is working in a normal range — not too active, not too quiet. It's included as part of a routine blood panel to screen for infection, inflammation, or any immune-related issues that could be affecting how you're feeling.

### HGB — Hemoglobin
Hemoglobin is the protein inside red blood cells that carries oxygen to every part of your body. When it's low, you have anemia — and fatigue, dizziness, and brain fog are classic signs. During perimenopause, heavy periods are a common cause of low hemoglobin, and those symptoms can be easily mistaken for menopause itself. This number helps tell the difference.

### HCT — Hematocrit
Hematocrit measures what percentage of your blood is made up of red blood cells. It works alongside hemoglobin to give a complete picture of whether you're anemic. If heavy perimenopausal bleeding has been reducing your red cell count over time, hematocrit will reflect that — and treating low iron or anemia can make a significant difference in how you feel day to day.

### PLT — Platelets
Platelets are tiny cell fragments that help your blood clot properly when you're injured. Most people's platelet count will be in a normal range and stay there. It's checked as part of a baseline because abnormal clotting can interact with certain treatments, including hormone therapy. It's more of a safety checkpoint than something most people need to worry about.

### FE — Serum Iron
Serum iron measures how much iron is actively circulating in your blood right now. Iron is essential for building hemoglobin — the protein that carries oxygen through your body. During perimenopause, heavy periods can steadily deplete iron levels, causing fatigue that gets blamed on menopause. This test helps identify whether low iron is contributing to how you're feeling.

### FER — Ferritin
Ferritin measures your stored iron — think of it as your iron reserve tank, separate from what's circulating in your blood right now. Here's what's important: ferritin drops before serum iron does. So you can feel exhausted and run-down from low iron stores while your other iron numbers still look normal. Low ferritin is one of the most commonly missed causes of fatigue in perimenopausal women.

### TIBC — Total Iron Binding Capacity
TIBC measures how much iron your blood proteins could carry if they were fully loaded. When iron stores are low, your body produces more carrier proteins to try to capture as much iron as possible — which shows up as a high TIBC. It's most useful when read alongside ferritin and serum iron, where together they tell a complete story about your iron status.

### B12 — Vitamin B12
B12 is essential for your nervous system, your red blood cells, and your overall energy metabolism. Deficiency creeps up gradually and can cause fatigue, brain fog, tingling in hands or feet, and low mood — symptoms that overlap heavily with menopause. Absorption of B12 naturally decreases as we age, so screening for it is especially worthwhile in your 40s and beyond.

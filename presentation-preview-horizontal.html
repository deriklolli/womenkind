Understanding Your Care Brings Peace of Mind

Knowing what's happening in your body — and why — makes the journey feel lighter. We've identified 5 areas to focus on together, each explained in plain language below.

01
Brain & Cognition  ·  1 of 5

Brain & Cognition

The brain fog and forgetfulness you're dealing with aren't signs that something is wrong with you mentally. They're a direct result of falling estrogen levels — a hormone deeply involved in how the brain manages energy, regulates neurotransmitters, and maintains the neural connections that allow for clear thinking and sharp recall.

Sarah, your intake scores and the way you described the word-finding issues really stood out to me. This is one of the most underrecognized effects of perimenopause — neurological, not psychological. We'll start with sleep optimization and cortisol reduction, which often provides meaningful relief within 6–8 weeks.

02
Vasomotor  ·  2 of 5

Nervous System & Vasomotor

Hot flashes and night sweats happen because your body's internal thermostat has become more sensitive. Changing estrogen levels affect the part of your brain that regulates temperature, causing it to overreact to small changes. This is the most common symptom of perimenopause — and there are highly effective treatments available.

Your Oura data shows you're waking between 2–4am consistently, and I suspect vasomotor events are the primary driver. I'd like to discuss low-dose transdermal estradiol — for women in your health profile, the benefit-to-risk ratio is very favorable.

03
Sleep  ·  3 of 5

Sleep & Circadian Rhythm

Sleep is one of the first things to shift as estrogen and progesterone begin to change — often before a person even connects the dots to hormones. These two hormones do quiet but significant work regulating the brain's sleep architecture, body temperature at night, and the signals that keep you asleep through early morning hours.

Your 30-day Oura average shows sleep efficiency of 74% — we want that closer to 85%. Micronized progesterone at bedtime is something I'd like to trial. Its calming, sleep-promoting effect is transformative for many of my patients.

04
Mood  ·  4 of 5

Mood & Mental Health

Mood changes during this time are not "just stress" — they have a real biological basis. Estrogen influences the brain chemicals that regulate mood, and as levels fluctuate you may experience anxiety, irritability, or a low that feels different from anything before. Recognizing this connection is the first step toward feeling like yourself again.

What you described — the sudden irritability and the feeling that your emotional baseline has shifted — is something I hear from almost every patient in perimenopause. It's hormonal, and it responds to treatment. Most women notice significant improvement within 4–8 weeks.

05
Hormonal  ·  5 of 5

Hormonal & Reproductive

At the core of everything you're experiencing is a natural shift in your hormone levels. Your ovaries are gradually producing less estrogen and progesterone, and your brain is sending stronger signals trying to compensate. Your lab results help us understand exactly where you are in this transition and how best to support you through it.

Your FSH of 42 and estradiol of 28 pg/mL confirm you're in perimenopause. This is exactly the window where early hormone therapy has the greatest long-term benefit — for your brain, heart, bones, and quality of life. Let's walk through your options at your next visit.

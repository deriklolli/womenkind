Brain & Cognition

You may have noticed changes in your thinking — moments of forgetfulness, difficulty finding words, or trouble focusing. This is your brain adjusting to changing hormone levels. Estrogen plays an important role in brain function, and as levels shift, these cognitive changes are common and expected.

JU

Sarah, your intake scores and the way you described the word-finding issues really stood out to me. This is one of the most underrecognized effects of perimenopause — it's neurological, not psychological. We'll start with optimizing sleep and reducing cortisol load, which often provides meaningful relief within 6–8 weeks. I want you to know this is very treatable.

Nervous System & Vasomotor

Hot flashes and night sweats happen because your body's internal thermostat has become more sensitive. Changing estrogen levels affect the part of your brain that regulates temperature, causing it to overreact to small changes. This is the most common symptom of menopause, and there are effective treatments available.

JU

Your Oura data shows you're waking between 2–4am consistently, and I suspect vasomotor events are the primary driver. I'd like to discuss low-dose transdermal estradiol — the evidence here is very strong, and for women in your age group and health profile, the benefit-to-risk ratio is highly favorable.

Sleep & Circadian Rhythm

If you're struggling with sleep, you're not alone — it's one of the most common concerns during this transition. Hormonal changes affect the brain chemicals that regulate your sleep-wake cycle, and night sweats can further disrupt rest. Improving sleep is often one of the first things we focus on because it impacts everything else.

JU

Your 30-day Oura average shows sleep efficiency of 74% — we want that closer to 85%. Beyond the vasomotor piece, I also noticed your REM is compressed. Micronized progesterone at bedtime is something I'd like to trial — it has a calming, sleep-promoting effect that many of my patients find transformative.

Mood & Mental Health

Mood changes during this time are not "just stress" — they have a real biological basis. Estrogen influences the brain chemicals that regulate mood, and as levels fluctuate, you may experience anxiety, irritability, or low mood that feels different from anything before. Recognizing this connection is the first step toward feeling more like yourself again.

JU

What you described in your intake — the sudden irritability and the feeling that your emotional baseline has shifted — is something I hear from almost every patient in perimenopause. It's hormonal, and it responds to treatment. As we stabilize estrogen levels, most women notice significant mood improvement within 4–8 weeks.

Hormonal & Reproductive

At the core of everything you're experiencing is a natural shift in your hormone levels. Your ovaries are gradually producing less estrogen and progesterone, and your brain is sending stronger signals (FSH) trying to compensate. Your lab results help us understand exactly where you are in this transition and how to best support you.

JU

Your FSH of 42 and estradiol of 28 pg/mL confirm you're in perimenopause. This is exactly the window where early hormone therapy has the greatest long-term benefit — for your brain, heart, bones, and quality of life.
